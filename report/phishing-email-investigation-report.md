# Phishing Email Investigation Report

## Incident Overview

**Project Title:** Phishing Email Investigation and Analysis

**Analyst:** Tatenda Tisha Lima

**Date of Analysis:** 19 July 2026

**Email Subject:** Important Notice: Action Required to Reclaim Your Funds

**Classification:** Suspected Phishing / Advance-Fee Fraud

**Risk Rating:** High

## Executive Summary
This investigation analyzed a suspicious email claiming that the recipient was entitled to receive previously withheld funds from multiple international financial institutions. The sender instructed the recipient to provide personally identifiable information (PII), including a government-issued identification document, to facilitate the release of the alleged funds.

The investigation included an examination of the email header, authentication results, sender information, and message content. Although the email successfully passed SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authentication, multiple indicators demonstrated that the message was consistent with a phishing campaign designed to harvest sensitive personal information through social engineering.

## Objectives
The objectives of this investigation were to:
- Determine whether the email exhibited characteristics of phishing.
- Analyze the technical email metadata.
- Identify social engineering techniques.
- Document Indicators of Compromise (IOCs).
- Assess the overall risk presented by the email.
- Provide security recommendations based on the findings.

## Scope
The investigation focused on:
- Email header information
- Sender identity
- Email authentication results
- Message content
- Social engineering indicators
- Requested information
- Overall threat assessment

No hyperlinks or attachments were opened during the investigation.

## Technical Analysis

### Email Authentication

| Control | Result | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| SPF | PASS | The sending server was authorized to send email on behalf of the sending domain. |
| DKIM | PASS | The message was cryptographically signed by the sending domain and was not modified in transit. |
| DMARC | PASS | Domain authentication requirements were successfully satisfied. |

### Interpretation
Passing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC confirms that the message originated from an authenticated account within the sending domain. These controls verify domain authentication and message integrity; they do not verify that the sender's claims are legitimate or that the content is trustworthy.

### Header Analysis
The email exhibited several characteristics commonly associated with phishing campaigns.

**Sender Identity**
The display name identified the sender as *[Redacted named individual]*, while the actual email address used an unrelated account name. This inconsistency reduces confidence in the claimed identity. Notably, the display name closely mirrored the recipient's own name, despite originating from an unrelated domain — a technique likely used to increase perceived familiarity or trust.

**Reply-To Address**
The Reply-To address differed from the sender address, indicating that responses would be directed to a different mailbox. This is a common tactic used to separate the visible sender identity from the destination receiving victim responses.

**Subject Line**
The subject line:
> Important Notice: Action Required to Reclaim Your Funds

uses urgency and the promise of financial gain to encourage immediate interaction.

## Content Analysis
The email claimed that funds linked to the recipient had been recovered following an international investigation involving multiple financial institutions.

The sender alleged that previous payment attempts had failed because another individual had fraudulently claimed ownership of the funds. The message then stated that all issues had been resolved and requested identity verification before payment could be released.

The email also impersonated a real Swiss financial institution (AEK Bank 1826), with the sender presenting themselves as its CEO/Chairman.

The requested information included:
- Full legal name
- Residential address
- Mobile telephone number
- Country, state and city
- Occupation
- Government-issued identification document

## Social Engineering Analysis
Several social engineering techniques were identified.

**1. Financial Incentive**
The email promised access to previously unavailable funds, exploiting the recipient's interest in receiving unexpected financial benefits.
Assessment: High Confidence

**2. Authority Impersonation**
The message impersonated a real Swiss financial institution and a named executive role within it, and referenced additional internationally recognized financial organizations to create further credibility. These additional organizations were not actually connected to the email and appear to have been name-dropped solely to increase perceived legitimacy.

Examples included:
- International Monetary Fund (IMF)
- World Bank
- Bank of America
- Royal Bank of Canada
- HDFC Bank

**3. Urgency**
The recipient was instructed to respond immediately to complete verification. Urgency is commonly used to reduce critical evaluation and encourage rapid compliance.

**4. Identity Harvesting**
The sender requested extensive personally identifiable information, including an identification document. The collection of this information could facilitate identity theft, financial fraud, or future targeted phishing attacks.

**5. Generic Greeting**
The message began with:
> Dear Beneficiary

Legitimate financial institutions generally identify customers by name rather than using generic salutations.

## Analyst Conclusion
The email demonstrates multiple characteristics consistent with a phishing campaign employing advance-fee fraud and identity harvesting techniques.

Although technical authentication checks (SPF, DKIM and DMARC) successfully validated the sending domain, these mechanisms authenticate the domain rather than the legitimacy of the sender's claims.

The combination of unsolicited financial promises, requests for sensitive personal information, inconsistent sender details, urgency, and authority-based persuasion provides strong evidence that the message is fraudulent.

No evidence was identified to support the legitimacy of the payment claims.

## Recommendations
- Do not respond to the sender.
- Do not provide any requested personal information.
- Do not open attachments or visit embedded links unless they have been independently verified.
- Report the email through the organization's phishing reporting process or email provider.
- Block the sender where appropriate.
- Monitor for similar phishing attempts using comparable themes.
- Provide phishing awareness training emphasizing that authenticated emails can still contain malicious content.

## Key Lessons Learned
This investigation demonstrates that email authentication mechanisms alone are insufficient for determining legitimacy. Effective phishing detection requires combining technical analysis with an assessment of sender behavior, message content, requested actions, and social engineering techniques.

A structured investigative approach enables analysts to distinguish between technically authentic emails and malicious campaigns designed to deceive recipients into disclosing sensitive information.
